%%  ABSTRACT
%% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
%% --- Part a: Problem domain ---
Rapid and often unregulated urban expansion in Indian metropolitan regions poses critical challenges for sustainable development, infrastructure planning, and environmental protection, particularly as encroachment on legally protected zones—coastal regulation zones (CRZ), forest reserves, and wetlands—accelerates.
%% --- Part b: Research approach with dataset ---
We present a five-pillar framework that integrates transfer learning-based patch classification, SAR-optical fusion, self-supervised pre-training, Bi-LSTM attention forecasting, and regulatory-aware encroachment alerting. Using Sentinel-2, Sentinel-1 SAR, and Landsat imagery spanning 1990--2023, we construct a multi-city Indian urban expansion dataset covering Mumbai, Delhi~NCR, and Bangalore (2,730 annotated patches) with automated labelling from ESA WorldCover~2021. A progressive three-stage fine-tuning strategy—classifier head only, partial backbone, full network—is applied to six models: SVM, Random Forest, MobileNetV3-Small, EfficientNet-B0, Swin-Tiny, and ResNet50.
%% --- Part c: Results ---
Our best model, ResNet50, achieves $97.5 \pm 0.2\%$ overall accuracy (OA) on real Indian satellite data (three-seed statistical validation), surpassing the published Indian SVM benchmark of 91.01\%. Even our optimised SVM baseline with spectral indices and grid search ($92.55 \pm 1.42\%$) exceeds published results, yet deep learning still outperforms it by five percentage points. We introduce the first Leave-One-City-Out (LOCO) cross-city benchmark for Indian metros, revealing an 18\% in-distribution-to-cross-city accuracy drop and a CNN-Transformer ranking reversal: ResNet50 wins in-distribution while Swin-Tiny generalises better (79.1 vs.\ 77.1\% LOCO OA). The integrated pipeline processes 21 real GeoTIFFs, builds city-level urban-area time series, drives a Bi-LSTM hybrid forecast ($R^2{=}0.9564$ standalone), and generates 55 forecast-conditioned encroachment alerts across seven Indian cities.
\end{abstract}

Urban expansion monitoring, transfer learning, satellite image classification, Indian metropolitan areas, cross-city generalisation, SAR-optical fusion, self-supervised learning, LSTM forecasting, encroachment alerting, Sentinel-2.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Problem Domain}

India's urban population is projected to reach 600~million by 2031, with major metropolitan regions experiencing 2--4$\times$ expansion in built-up area since 1990~\cite{census2011}. This rapid urbanisation creates three interlinked challenges that are difficult to address without automated satellite monitoring.

\textbf{Encroachment on protected zones.} Indian environmental law designates several overlapping protection regimes: Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) Notification~2019~\cite{crz2019}, Forest Conservation Act~1980~\cite{fca1980}, and Wetland Rules~2017~\cite{wetland2017}. Violations are common but costly to detect manually. Mumbai's Sanjay Gandhi National Park, Bangalore's Bellandur wetland, and Delhi's Yamuna floodplain are routinely encroached upon by informal construction that satellite imagery can reveal months before ground inspection.

\textbf{Infrastructure planning under uncertainty.} Urban growth models used for road, metro, and water-supply planning rely on point predictions. Without uncertainty quantification, planners cannot distinguish a robust forecast from a fragile one. Overconfident growth models have caused misallocated capital investment in Indian smart cities~\cite{smartcity}.

\textbf{Limited operational deployment of deep learning.} Despite strong benchmark accuracy on datasets such as EuroSAT~\cite{helber2019} (up to 99\%), deep learning methods for Indian urban classification remain rare in operational use. Published studies predominantly rely on traditional machine learning (SVM, Random Forest) on single cities~\cite{chamoli2024}, achieving 89--91\% OA, and none provides cross-city transfer evaluation or connects classification to downstream forecasting and alerting.

Satellite data from Sentinel-2 (10\,m, 2015--present), Sentinel-1 SAR (10\,m, 2015--present), and Landsat~5/7/8/9 (30\,m, 1990--present) provides the spatial and temporal coverage needed to address these challenges. Google Earth Engine (GEE) enables cloud-masked, speckle-filtered composites at continental scale. Yet the pipeline from raw imagery to actionable regulatory alerts has never been assembled for Indian cities in a single reproducible framework.

\subsection{Key Contributions}

This paper makes five contributions.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Multi-city Indian urban expansion benchmark with LOCO evaluation.} We construct 2,730 annotated Sentinel-2 patches from Mumbai, Delhi~NCR, and Bangalore (2017--2023), with labels from ESA WorldCover~2021. We introduce the first Leave-One-City-Out (LOCO) cross-city transfer benchmark for Indian metros, reporting mean~$\pm$~std over three random seeds (42, 123, 7) for all key experiments.

  \item \textbf{Progressive fine-tuning achieving 97.5\% OA without domain-specific pre-training.} A three-stage strategy—classifier head only, partial backbone, full network—with combined loss (CE~+~Focal~+~Dice) achieves $97.5 \pm 0.2\%$ OA using ImageNet-pretrained ResNet50, competitive with published state of the art on Indian urban data.

  \item \textbf{Novel 3-class taxonomy with Transition buffer.} Instead of binary urban/non-urban labelling used in all published Indian studies, we define a \textit{Transition} class via 100\,m morphological dilation of urban boundaries. This absorbs mixed-pixel ambiguity at urban edges and is the class most relevant to encroachment detection.

  \item \textbf{Uncertainty-aware Bi-LSTM forecasting with MC Dropout.} The temporal module provides 95\% confidence intervals on urban area forecasts (2024--2035) via 50 Monte Carlo forward passes, enabling risk-aware infrastructure planning—absent from all published Indian urban growth models.

  \item \textbf{End-to-end pipeline: classify $\to$ time series $\to$ forecast $\to$ regulatory alert.} No published work connects all four stages for Indian urban expansion. The pipeline encodes 10 India-specific regulatory zone types with 30+ named protected areas and automatically routes alerts to the relevant authority (MoEFCC, CZMA, State Forest Department).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Transfer Learning for Earth Observation}

Deep transfer learning from ImageNet-pretrained backbones is now standard for satellite image classification. On EuroSAT (27,000 Sentinel-2 patches, 10 classes), ResNet50 achieves 96.78\%~OA~\cite{he2016}, EfficientNet-B3 reaches 97.1\%~\cite{tan2019}, and Vision Transformers push to 98.0\%~\cite{dosovitskiy2021}. The original EuroSAT benchmark reported 98.57\% using all 13 spectral bands~\cite{helber2019}. However, these results are obtained on curated European patches; transferability to uncurated Indian metropolitan imagery with distinct urban morphologies remains largely unexplored.

\subsection{Indian Urban Classification}

Indian urban classification studies predominantly rely on traditional machine learning. Chamoli~et~al.~\cite{chamoli2024} achieved 91.01\%~OA with SVM and 89.67\% with Random Forest on Sentinel-2 imagery of Uttarakhand—the highest published SVM result on Indian satellite data. The highest reported Indian urban accuracy is IRUNet ensemble (98.21\%) by Katpadi~et~al.~\cite{katpadi2025} on Tamil Nadu; however, this is binary pixel-level segmentation on a single region—a fundamentally easier task than our 3-class multi-city patch classification with LOCO evaluation. No published Indian study evaluates cross-city transfer or reports multi-seed statistical validation.

\subsection{Cross-City Domain Adaptation and Transfer}

Cross-city transfer in remote sensing has been studied on European and Chinese cities. HighDAN~\cite{li2023} performs cross-city semantic segmentation via domain adaptation on the C2Seg benchmark (Berlin--Augsburg, Beijing--Wuhan). Wang~et~al.~\cite{wang2023} evaluate deep transfer learning across Chinese cities. These works focus on adaptation techniques; we instead quantify raw cross-city generalisation gaps without adaptation, providing the first such baselines for Indian metros. A 2025 systematic review of 89 Sentinel-2 studies found that geographic transferability reduces accuracy by 15--25\% from benchmark to operational deployment~\cite{mdpi2025}; our 18\% LOCO gap independently confirms this range for Indian metros.

\subsection{Change Detection}

Siamese architectures for bi-temporal change detection have advanced rapidly on LEVIR-CD: Siamese-UNet (F1\,=\,87.14\%), STCD Siamese Transformer (F1\,=\,89.85\%)~\cite{stcd2022}, SMDNet with diffusion models (F1\,=\,90.99\%)~\cite{smdnet2024}, and GAS-Net (F1\,=\,91.21\%)~\cite{gasnet2023}. Our framework extends beyond detection to forecasting and regulatory alerting.

\subsection{CNN vs.\ Transformer Generalisation}

Bai~et~al.~\cite{bai2021} demonstrated that transformers substantially outperform CNNs on out-of-distribution samples, attributing this to the dynamic weight computation of self-attention. Applied Intelligence~(2024)~\cite{appliedintel2024} and Springer Big~Data~(2023)~\cite{springerbigdata2023} further show that CNNs outperform ViTs on small datasets due to stronger inductive biases—consistent with our in-distribution finding that ResNet50 (11.3M params) outperforms Swin-Tiny (29.8M params) with only 2,730 training patches.

\subsection{Urban Growth Forecasting}

CA-Markov and hybrid models dominate Indian urban growth forecasting~\cite{camarkov2025,lucknow2025,caann2025}, achieving OA up to 93.6\%. However, all published Indian forecasting studies produce point predictions without uncertainty quantification. Our Bi-LSTM with MC Dropout provides 95\% confidence intervals—a capability absent from all compared works.

\section{Dataset Characteristics and Tools}

\subsection{Study Area}

We focus on three Indian metropolitan regions representing different urbanisation archetypes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Mumbai:} Coastal megacity; constrained by the Arabian Sea and Sanjay Gandhi National Park; dense informal settlement growth.
  \item \textbf{Delhi~NCR:} Radial sprawl with satellite towns (Gurgaon, Noida, Faridabad); gradual urban-rural fringe.
  \item \textbf{Bangalore:} IT-corridor-led expansion with dispersed built-up nuclei; rapid peripheral growth since 2000.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Satellite Data}

\textbf{Sentinel-2 L2A (Optical, 10\,m).} Six bands (B2, B3, B4, B8, B11, B12) from 2017--2023. Cloud-masked using the Scene Classification Layer (SCL), seasonally composited (pre-monsoon: January--March) to minimise cloud cover. GEE asset: \texttt{COPERNICUS/S2\_SR\_HARMONIZED}~\cite{sentinel2}.

\textbf{Sentinel-1 SAR (10\,m).} VV and VH polarisation, 2023 post-monsoon. Speckle-filtered using focal median ($3{\times}3$). GEE asset: \texttt{COPERNICUS/S1\_GRD}~\cite{sentinel1}. Normalised to dB scale with clip $[-30, 0]$\,dB.

\textbf{Landsat 5/7/8/9 (30\,m).} Harmonised 6-band composites for 1990, 2000, 2010, and 2023, used exclusively for Pillar~IV temporal modelling. Auto-selection of optimal Landsat sensor per year with band harmonisation. GEE assets: \texttt{LANDSAT/L*\_C02/T1\_L2}~\cite{landsat}.

\subsection{Labelling and 3-Class Taxonomy}

Labels are derived from ESA WorldCover~2021~\cite{zanaga2022} (10\,m) cross-validated with Google Dynamic World~\cite{brown2022}. We define three classes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Urban:} WorldCover class~50 (Built-up).
  \item \textbf{Non-Urban:} All other WorldCover classes (vegetation, water, bare soil).
  \item \textbf{Transition:} 100\,m morphological dilation of Urban boundaries—captures pixels at the active urban-rural interface where expansion occurs.
\end{itemize}

The Transition class is novel for Indian urban studies; all published comparators use binary Urban/Non-Urban labelling. It absorbs mixed-pixel ambiguity at 10\,m resolution and is the most application-critical class for encroachment detection.

\textbf{Dataset statistics:} 2,730 patches ($256{\times}256$ pixels, 10\,m) across three cities; class distribution: Urban~19.2\%, Non-Urban~62.4\%, Transition~18.4\%. Geographic train/val/test split (70/15/15) with seven LOCO folds. SAR patches extracted at matching locations: 910 of 2,730 optical patches have paired SAR coverage.

\subsection{External Benchmark: LEVIR-CD}

For Siamese change detection evaluation, we use LEVIR-CD~\cite{chen2020levir}: 637 bi-temporal image pairs ($1024{\times}1024$, 0.5\,m GSD) covering Wuhan and Changsha with building change labels. Used exclusively for Pillar~V change-detector training.

\subsection{Socio-Economic Features (Pillar~IV)}

Each city-year time step is augmented with: Census population (2001, 2011 with intercensal interpolation)~\cite{census2011}, GSDP~\cite{rbi}, Smart City Mission allocations~\cite{smartcity}, AMRUT funds, metro rail length, NH density, SEZ/IT park count, FSI green cover percentage, and policy event indicators (Liberalisation~1991, JNNURM~2005, Smart~City~2015, RERA~2016, COVID~2020).

\subsection{Tools and Infrastructure}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Data:} Google Earth Engine (Python API v0.1.374), Google Drive for Desktop ($\sim$5.7\,GB GeoTIFF exports).
  \item \textbf{Deep learning:} PyTorch~2.0, torchvision~0.15, timm~0.9.
  \item \textbf{Classical ML:} scikit-learn~1.3 (SVM, RF, Ridge, PCA, GridSearchCV).
  \item \textbf{Analysis:} matplotlib, seaborn, scipy (paired t-test), pandas.
  \item \textbf{Hardware:} NVIDIA GeForce RTX~4070 Laptop GPU (8\,GB VRAM), 16\,GB RAM, Windows~11.
  \item \textbf{Seeds:} 42, 123, 7 (all stochastic experiments repeated three times; mean~$\pm$~std reported throughout).
\end{itemize}

\section{Proposed Methodology}

Pillar~I adds SAR modality; Pillar~II adds self-supervised pre-training.}

\subsection{Backbone Architectures}

We evaluate four deep learning backbones with identical downstream heads:

\textbf{ResNet50}~\cite{he2016} (11.3M parameters): Standard residual network. Our primary backbone; achieves best accuracy ($97.5 \pm 0.2\%$) and lowest variance across seeds (std~=~0.2\%).

\textbf{EfficientNet-B0}~\cite{tan2019} (5.3M): Compound-scaled efficient CNN. Used as ablation backbone for controlled experiments.

\textbf{Swin-Tiny}~\cite{liu2021} (29.8M): Hierarchical transformer with shifted windows. Best cross-city generaliser ($79.1 \pm 3.9\%$ LOCO OA).

\textbf{MobileNetV3-Small}~\cite{howard2019} (3.4M): Lightweight NAS-designed CNN. Smallest deployment footprint (30.5\,MB GPU memory, 184.4\,patches/sec).

\subsection{Feature Pyramid Network (FPN)}

A three-level Feature Pyramid Network~\cite{lin2017fpn} aggregates multi-scale features (strides 4, 8, 16) from the backbone. All FPN outputs are upsampled to a common resolution and concatenated before the classification head. The ablation (\cref{tab:ablation}) shows a +0.3\% OA benefit, primarily aiding Transition class boundary detection.

\subsection{Progressive Fine-Tuning}

Training proceeds in three stages with cosine learning-rate annealing and early stopping (patience = 10):

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Stage 1 — Classifier head only} (5 epochs, lr~$=10^{-3}$): All backbone weights frozen.
  \item \textbf{Stage 2 — Last two backbone blocks} (5 epochs, lr~$=10^{-4}$): Gradual thawing.
  \item \textbf{Stage 3 — Full network} (5 epochs, lr~$=10^{-5}$): End-to-end fine-tuning.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Combined Loss Function}

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = 0.6 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + 0.3 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{Focal}}(\gamma{=}2) + 0.1 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{Dice}}
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}

Focal loss~\cite{lin2017focal} down-weights easy examples; Dice loss improves Transition class recall. Class weights $[1.0, 1.0, 3.0]$ (Urban, Non-Urban, Transition) are applied to $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{Focal}}$.

\subsection{Pillar I: SAR-Optical Fusion}

A dual-branch encoder processes optical (6-channel Sentinel-2) and SAR (2-channel Sentinel-1) inputs separately, then fuses their feature maps via concatenation before the shared FPN and classification head. Trained on 910 aligned optical-SAR patch pairs; compared against optical-only baseline with the same backbone (EfficientNet-B0).

\subsection{Pillar II: Self-Supervised Pre-Training (SimCLR)}

We apply SimCLR~\cite{chen2020simclr} contrastive pre-training on unlabelled Indian satellite patches (random crop, colour jitter, Gaussian blur augmentations; projection head dimension 128; temperature $\tau{=}0.07$; 100 epochs). The pre-trained backbone is then fine-tuned with the progressive strategy and compared against an ImageNet-initialised baseline.

\subsection{Pillar IV: Bi-LSTM Temporal Forecasting}

The forecasting module is a Bi-directional LSTM~\cite{hochreiter1997} with residual connections, LayerNorm, multi-head temporal attention (2 heads), and MC Dropout ($p{=}0.2$) for uncertainty quantification. Model size: 94K parameters. Inputs per time step: satellite-derived urban area (sq\,km) + 15 socio-economic and policy features. Temporal split: train 1990--2015, validation 2016--2019, test 2020--2023. Uncertainty: 50 MC forward passes yield 95\% confidence intervals.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Integrated Urban Expansion Pipeline}
\label{alg:pipeline}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE GeoTIFFs $\{G_1, \ldots, G_N\}$, trained ResNet50 $\theta^*$, socio-economic features $\mathbf{s}$
\ENSURE Alerts $\mathcal{A}$ with severity and authority routing
\FOR{each GeoTIFF $G_i$}
  \STATE Slide $256{\times}256$ window, batch-infer with $\theta^*$
  \STATE Count Urban pixels $\to$ area $a_{c,y}$ for city $c$, year $y$
\ENDFOR
\STATE Build time series $\mathbf{u}_c = [a_{c,1990}, \ldots, a_{c,2023}]$
\STATE Hybrid override: replace synthetic anchors with real $a_{c,y}$
\STATE Run Bi-LSTM + MC Dropout $\to$ $\hat{a}_{c,t \in [2024,2035]}$ with 95\% CI
\FOR{each forecast zone $z$ in city $c$}
  \IF{$z$ overlaps regulatory protected area $\mathcal{R}$}
    \STATE Compute severity $\in \{\text{LOW, MED, HIGH, CRIT}\}$
    \STATE Route alert to authority($\mathcal{R}$)
    \STATE Append alert to $\mathcal{A}$
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $\mathcal{A}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Pillar V: Regulatory Encroachment Alert Engine}

A three-head neural change detector classifies each patch as: (1) changed / unchanged (binary), (2) severity (NONE / LOW / MEDIUM / HIGH / CRITICAL), and (3) encroachment type (CRZ violation, forest encroachment, lake encroachment, etc.). The system encodes 10 India-specific regulatory zone types with 30+ named protected areas across seven cities, and automatically routes alerts to the correct authority: MoEFCC for forests, CZMA for coastal zones, State Forest Department for protected forests.

\textbf{CNN-Transformer ranking reversal.} The model that wins in-distribution (ResNet50, 97.5\%) loses on cross-city (ResNet50 drops 20.4\%), while the model that appears weaker in-distribution (Swin-Tiny, 93.6\%) generalises better (Swin-Tiny drops only 14.5\%). This 5.9\% generalisation premium for self-attention confirms the findings of Bai~et~al.~\cite{bai2021}: CNN filters learn city-specific textures (Mumbai's coastal patterns, Delhi's road grids) that do not transfer, while Swin-Tiny's self-attention learns more abstract, transferable urban features.

\textbf{18\% domain gap.} Average accuracy drops from 97.5\% (in-distribution) to 79.1\% (LOCO)—an 18.4\% gap that independently falls within the 15--25\% range reported by a 2025 systematic review of 89 Sentinel-2 studies~\cite{mdpi2025}.

\textbf{Per-city difficulty.} Delhi~NCR is the hardest held-out target (71.8--76.7\%~OA across models) due to gradual radial sprawl creating wide urban-rural gradients. Bangalore is the easiest held-out target (80.4--83.9\%) despite being the hardest in-distribution city (80.5\%~OA caused by IT-park vegetation fragments triggering Non-Urban~$\to$~Transition confusion). This separation between \textit{local ambiguity} and \textit{cross-city domain shift} is itself a novel finding.

\textbf{SAR fusion (Pillar~I).} SAR fusion ($87.9\%$) underperforms optical-only ($96.7\%$) due to limited pairing (33\% of patches have SAR) and season mismatch (post-monsoon SAR vs.\ pre-monsoon optical). With sufficient labelled data, practitioners should prefer ImageNet initialisation over SSL pre-training.

\subsection{Pillar IV: Urban Expansion Forecasting}

In the controlled standalone benchmark, the Bi-LSTM achieves $R^2{=}0.9564$, MAE~$= 119.53$\,km$^2$, MAPE~$= 6.66\%$. In the integrated pipeline (real satellite-derived anchor years), the task is substantially harder: $R^2{=}0.5590$, MAE~$= 330.35$\,km$^2$. The Ridge regression linear baseline achieves $R^2{=}0.6006$ in the integrated setting—the LSTM's advantage is uncertainty quantification and non-linear policy event modelling, not aggregate accuracy. Forecasts for 2024--2035 include 95\% confidence intervals via MC Dropout, which—to our knowledge—no published Indian urban forecasting study provides.

\subsection{Pillar V: Regulatory Encroachment Alerts}

\section{Conclusion}

We presented a five-pillar urban expansion monitoring framework evaluated on real Indian satellite data from Mumbai, Delhi~NCR, and Bangalore, with the following findings. (1)~Progressive fine-tuning of ImageNet-pretrained ResNet50 achieves $97.5 \pm 0.2\%$~OA, competitive with published state of the art without domain-specific pre-training. (2)~Even an optimised SVM ($92.55\%$) surpasses the published Indian SVM benchmark (91.01\%), yet deep learning outperforms it by five points. (3)~A CNN-Transformer ranking reversal emerges under cross-city transfer—ResNet50 wins in-distribution, Swin-Tiny generalises better under LOCO evaluation. (4)~The 18\% in-distribution-to-LOCO gap confirms, for Indian metros specifically, the 15--25\% operational deploy gap reported in recent systematic reviews. (5)~The 3-class taxonomy with Transition buffer and uncertainty-aware MC Dropout forecasting add methodological value absent from prior Indian urban studies. (6)~The executed integration pipeline processed 21 real GeoTIFFs, built multi-year city time series, produced hybrid 2024--2035 forecasts, and generated 55 forecast-conditioned simulated alerts across seven cities.

\textbf{Future work} includes expanding to Tier-2/Tier-3 Indian cities, validating alert outputs against ground-truth encroachment records, and improving SAR fusion through temporal alignment and multi-orbit integration.

\section{Reproducibility Protocol}
\label{appendix:repro}

\textbf{Hardware:} NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4070 Laptop GPU (8\,GB VRAM), 16\,GB RAM, Windows~11.

\textbf{Software:} Python~3.11, PyTorch~2.0.1+cu118, torchvision~0.15.2, timm~0.9.12, scikit-learn~1.3.2, earthengine-api~0.1.374.

\textbf{Seeds:} 42, 123, 7. Set via \texttt{torch.manual\_seed}, \texttt{numpy.random.seed}, and \texttt{random.seed}; CUDA determinism enabled with \texttt{torch.backends.cudnn.deterministic = True}.

\textbf{Hyperparameters:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Batch size: 32; image size: $256{\times}256$; channels: 6 (S2: B2,B3,B4,B8,B11,B12).
  \item Learning rates: Stage 1: $10^{-3}$; Stage 2: $10^{-4}$; Stage 3: $10^{-5}$. Cosine annealing, patience~10.
  \item Loss weights: CE~0.6, Focal~0.3 ($\gamma{=}2$), Dice~0.1. Class weights: [1.0, 1.0, 3.0].
  \item Augmentation: RandomHorizontalFlip, RandomVerticalFlip, RandomRotation(90°), ColorJitter, Mixup ($\alpha{=}0.2$).
  \item SVM improved: RBF kernel, $C \in \{0.1,1,10,100\}$, $\gamma \in \{0.001,0.01,0.1\}$; StandardScaler + PCA (99\% variance); spectral indices: NDVI, NDBI, NDWI, SAVI, BSI.
  \item Bi-LSTM: hidden\_dim~64, 1~layer, 2-head attention, MC Dropout~0.2, Huber loss, Adam ($5 \times 10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-3}$), 50~epochs.
  \item SimCLR: temperature~0.07, projection~128-dim, 100~epochs; Adam $3 \times 10^{-4}$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Data access:} GEE Python API with project \texttt{urban-expansion-india}. GEE script at \texttt{src/gee\_export\_script.js}. Processed patches: \texttt{data/indian\_cities\_locked/\{city\}/patches/}.

\textbf{Code:} All source code, trained model checkpoints, and paper-ready figures are publicly available at \url{https://github.com/kaustubhagarwal21/urban-expansion-monitoring}. Data available at \url{https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/1mmgGRtjHyVpQLMTSQE8xUOFv1gslrpWv}.
